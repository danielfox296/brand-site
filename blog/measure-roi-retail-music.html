How to Measure the ROI of In-Store Music

Daniel Fox · March 2026 · 7 min read

Measuring the return on investment of in-store music requires isolating audio's contribution to five retail metrics: dwell time, conversion rate, average basket size, customer satisfaction scores, and repeat visit rate. Most retailers cannot do this today. Not because the metrics are exotic—every competent retail operation already tracks at least three of the five—but because their music systems provide no data to correlate against. The audio is unparameterized, unlogged, and unmeasured. This article provides a framework for building a measurement practice, whether you use Entuned, a legacy provider, or manage music in-house.

Why Most Retailers Cannot Measure Music's Impact

The problem is structural, not technical. Traditional background music systems—Mood Media, Soundtrack Your Brand, SiriusXM for Business, or a store manager's Spotify account—do not log what played, when it played, or what musical characteristics it had. Without this input data, there is nothing to correlate against output data (sales, traffic, satisfaction). You have a dependent variable (revenue) and no independent variable (musical parameters). That is not a measurement gap. That is the absence of a measurement system entirely.

The few retailers who have attempted to measure music's impact typically run crude A/B tests: "jazz week" vs. "pop week," then compare sales. These tests fail for three reasons. The conditions are not controlled (weather, promotions, staffing all vary). The musical variable is defined at the genre level, which is too coarse to be actionable. And sample sizes of one or two weeks per condition produce statistically meaningless results. The conclusion is usually "we couldn't tell" or "it didn't matter"—which is a failure of the experimental design, not evidence that music does not matter.

Peer-reviewed research tells a different story. Milliman's 1982 study demonstrated that tempo alone shifts dwell time by measurable amounts. Areni and Kim (1993) showed an 8–12% increase in willingness to pay when musical style was congruent with product positioning. Chebat and Michon (2003) found that ambient audio independently shapes quality perception. The effects are real. The problem is that retailers lack the instrumentation to capture them in operational settings.

The Five Metrics That Matter

Dwell time. The duration a customer spends in the store or in a specific zone. This is the most extensively studied metric in retail audio research. Tempo directly entrains motor behavior: slower music produces longer visits. Measurement requires foot traffic analytics (camera-based, Wi-Fi, or LiDAR) at sufficient granularity to detect changes of 5–15% in average visit duration.

Conversion rate. The percentage of visitors who make a purchase. This is the metric that separates "people stayed longer" from "people stayed longer and bought something." Dwell time without conversion is a cost, not a benefit. A well-designed audio environment should move both. Measurement requires POS data correlated with traffic counts.

Average basket size. The mean transaction value. Music that increases perceived quality or creates congruence between the audio environment and premium products can shift basket size upward. The Areni and Kim finding on willingness to pay operates through this mechanism. Measurement requires POS data, segmented by time period and ideally by zone.

Customer satisfaction scores. NPS, CSAT, or equivalent survey data. Audio environment is consistently identified in customer experience research as a contributor to overall atmosphere perception, which in turn drives satisfaction and loyalty metrics. Measurement requires survey deployment with sufficient sample sizes to detect changes in the 3–8 point range.

Repeat visit rate. The frequency with which existing customers return. This is the longest-lead metric and the hardest to isolate, but potentially the highest-value. A store that feels right—where the sensory environment matches the customer's identity and expectations—generates habitual return visits. Measurement requires loyalty program data or persistent traffic identification.

How to Build a Measurement Framework

Step one: establish a baseline. For at least 30 days before changing anything about the audio environment, capture daily figures for each metric you intend to track. This baseline must account for day-of-week and time-of-day variation. A Monday baseline is not comparable to a Saturday intervention.

Step two: define the intervention. "We changed the music" is not a measurable intervention. You need to specify what changed: tempo range, genre, volume level, time-of-day scheduling, or—if using a parameterized system like Entuned—which of the 31 flow factors were adjusted and by how much.

Step three: control for confounds. The most reliable approach is A/B testing across matched locations. If you operate 20+ stores, designate half as treatment (new audio) and half as control (existing audio) for a defined period. Single-store before/after comparisons are weaker but viable if the test period is long enough (60+ days per condition) and seasonal effects are controlled.

Step four: calculate statistical significance. A 2% lift in conversion rate is not meaningful if your sample size cannot distinguish it from noise. For a typical retail location processing 500 transactions per day, you need approximately 14 days of data per condition to detect a 3% change in conversion rate at 95% confidence. Smaller effects require longer test periods or more locations.

Step five: compute the dollar impact. If average basket size increases by $2.40 across 500 daily transactions, that is $1,200/day or $36,000/month per location. If the audio system costs $500/month, the ROI is 71:1. These numbers are illustrative, but they demonstrate why precision measurement matters: even small effects on high-volume metrics produce large dollar returns.

What If You Are Not Ready for a Full Measurement Program?

Start with two metrics: dwell time and conversion rate. These are the most responsive to audio changes, the easiest to measure, and the most directly connected to revenue. If you do not have foot traffic analytics, start there—the investment pays for itself across multiple operational use cases beyond audio. If you do have traffic data, you already have the denominator. The music intervention provides the variable. The POS provides the result.

The most expensive option is not the one that costs the most per month. It is the one that costs anything per month and gives you no data on what you are getting for it. A $30/month playlist with no measurement is not cheap. It is unaccountable. The first step in measuring ROI is choosing a system that produces something to measure.
