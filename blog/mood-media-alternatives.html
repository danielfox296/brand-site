Mood Media Alternatives in 2026: What Retail Leaders Are Switching To

Daniel Fox · March 2026 · 9 min read

Mood Media is a Dallas-based commercial music and in-store experience company, formed through the 2011 acquisition of Muzak by Mood Media Corporation. It serves approximately 500,000 business locations across 140 countries, making it one of the largest providers of background music, digital signage, and scent marketing to retail, hospitality, and foodservice operators. Its core product is a catalog-based music service: licensed recordings organized into branded channels or custom playlists and distributed via proprietary hardware to commercial locations under multi-year contracts. In 2026, a growing number of retail operators are actively evaluating alternatives. The reasons are structural, not incidental — and understanding them requires a clear look at what the market now offers.

Why Are Retailers Looking for Alternatives?

Mood Media was built for a different era of retail. The catalog-and-playlist model it operates on was the only viable model when it was developed, and for a long time it was adequate. Music played. Silence was filled. Customers heard branded content. That was enough.

What has changed is the standard retail operators now apply to every channel in their environment. Digital marketing is tracked to conversion. Visual merchandising is A/B tested. Staffing models are tied to traffic data. In-store audio has not kept pace with this shift — and when finance teams ask what the music budget is producing, most legacy providers cannot answer the question.

Five specific friction points appear consistently in operator conversations:

Long-term contract structures. Mood Media contracts are typically three to five years with early termination penalties. For a mid-size retailer, this creates meaningful switching costs and reduces the leverage to renegotiate on service quality or pricing.

Absence of measurement. The service logs what is scheduled to play, but not what business outcomes the music influenced. There is no dwell-time correlation, no conversion attribution, no behavioral dataset. The music is an input with no tracked output.

No AI or behavioral data layer. Targeting is done by genre, era, or mood channel — categories defined by musical style, not by customer psychology or behavioral objectives. There is no mechanism to translate psychographic data about your customer into a musical specification, and no feedback loop to optimize performance over time.

Catalog repetition. Even a large licensed library is a fixed set. Playlists rotate. Employees hear the same tracks dozens of times per month, which creates ambient fatigue and degrades the in-store environment for your highest-frequency staff. Frequent customers notice repetition too.

Pricing opacity. Mood Media pricing is typically bundled with hardware installation, on-site support, and optional add-ons (scent, digital signage), making per-location music costs difficult to isolate. Operators frequently discover that the effective cost per location per month is considerably higher than the headline figure quoted at signing.

How the Major Alternatives Compare

The market has segmented into four distinct tiers, each representing a different architectural approach. Here is how the leading providers position and differentiate.

Soundtrack Your Brand

The Swedish-founded platform is the clearest example of what a modern, SaaS-native catalog service looks like. Soundtrack Your Brand operates on a licensed catalog model with a clean web interface, no proprietary hardware requirement, and a subscription structure without multi-year lock-in. Pricing runs approximately $34.99–$49.99 per location per month, which includes blanket licensing for the US and most of Europe.

It is well-regarded for playlist quality and UX. What it does not offer is an AI optimization layer or behavioral measurement. Targeting remains genre- and mood-based. There is no mechanism to generate music to specification, correlate audio to retail outcomes, or close a feedback loop between what played and what happened on the floor. For retailers migrating off Mood Media primarily for pricing or contract flexibility, Soundtrack Your Brand is a reasonable landing point. For retailers who want to treat audio as a performance channel, the ceiling is the same.

Cloud Cover Music

Cloud Cover Music is a US-based, SMB-focused licensed music service. Pricing starts at $9.99–$24.99 per location per month, making it the lowest-cost option among legitimate commercial providers. It handles ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC licensing and offers a browser-based player with no hardware dependency.

The trade-off for the price is scope. Cloud Cover is designed for single-location businesses or small multi-unit operators who need compliant background music without the overhead of enterprise contracts. It does not offer psychographic targeting, multi-zone configuration, behavioral data, or AI-generated content. At scale, the operational limitations become visible quickly. A 30-location retailer will find the platform underpowered for anything beyond basic music compliance.

SiriusXM for Business

SiriusXM for Business is the commercial licensing arm of SiriusXM, offering access to a large library of stations and curated channels for business locations. It targets mid-market and enterprise accounts, often bundled with existing SiriusXM broadcast relationships.

The catalog is deep — 200+ channels across every genre — and the brand carries inherent recognition in retail conversations. The infrastructure is legacy, however. SiriusXM for Business operates on the same playlist-and-channel model as its consumer product, without meaningful AI optimization or behavioral measurement capability. Pricing is typically negotiated as part of broader AV or satellite contracts and is not publicly listed. Market estimates place enterprise accounts in the $30–80 per location per month range depending on volume and bundled services.

QSIC

QSIC is an Australian-founded platform that describes itself as an AI-powered retail audio solution. It is the closest analog to Entuned in terms of positioning, with stated capabilities around behavioral targeting and performance measurement. Its primary market is Australia and New Zealand, where it has documented deployments with QSR and retail chains.

QSIC's approach is AI-adjacent rather than fully generative: the system uses AI to select and sequence from a licensed catalog rather than generating original music from scratch. This preserves the catalog model's constraints around repetition and licensing cost while adding a modest intelligence layer for scheduling optimization. North American availability is limited, and the pricing model is not publicly disclosed for enterprise accounts. For US and European retailers, QSIC is more of a reference point for where the market is heading than a near-term operational alternative.

The platform uses AI-powered retail music optimization to generate original music from scratch, to specification, for each deployment. There is no licensed catalog, no playlist, and no repetition. Every composition is new.

The targeting mechanism is distinct. What Questions Should You Ask Any Vendor?

Before signing with any in-store music provider — whether you are leaving Mood Media or evaluating for the first time — these five questions will separate vendors who can deliver measurable value from those who cannot.

1. What do you measure, and what can I report on?
Any provider can tell you what is scheduled to play. Very few can tell you what behavioral outcomes the music influenced. Ask for a specific list of the metrics available in the reporting dashboard. If the answer is limited to play counts and schedule adherence, the platform does not have a measurement architecture.

2. What are the full contract terms, including exit provisions?
Ask for the early termination clause in writing before the sales conversation advances. Multi-year contracts with penalty clauses are common in legacy services and substantially raise the real cost of switching. A provider confident in its performance outcomes should not require multi-year lock-in to protect its revenue.

3. Is the music generated or licensed — and who bears the licensing liability?
This question surfaces the true cost of licensed music. Catalog services either pass through PRO fees (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC) directly or embed them in subscription pricing. For a 50-location retailer, this can add $30,000–$60,000 annually on top of the software fee. AI-generated services with no catalog have no licensing exposure. Understand what you are actually paying for.

4. How is the music targeted to my specific customer profile?
Genre and mood labels are demographic proxies, not behavioral specifications. Ask the vendor to explain the mechanism by which your customer psychographic data influences what plays in your store. If the answer is "you pick a channel," you are buying a playlist. If the answer involves a translatable data layer between customer psychology and musical parameters, you are buying targeting capability.

5. Can you show documented ROI from a comparable retailer?
Not a headline. Not a testimonial. A documented before-and-after comparison across specific metrics — dwell time, conversion rate, average basket size — at a retailer of similar format, customer base, and market position. If the vendor cannot produce this, they have not built the measurement infrastructure to generate it. That is itself the answer to your first question.

The Bottom Line

The alternatives to Mood Media in 2026 range from lighter-weight licensed services (Soundtrack Your Brand, Cloud Cover) to legacy broadcast platforms (SiriusXM for Business) to AI-adjacent sequencers (QSIC) to fully generative systems with a behavioral data layer (Entuned). Mood Media was not built badly — it was built for an era when filling silence with brand-appropriate music was sufficient. That era is ending.

The question for retail leaders is not which service has the best playlist. It is which service can answer the question: what did the music do for the business this quarter? That question eliminates most of the market. For operators who intend to hold in-store audio to the same performance standard as every other channel, the shift to AI-powered retail music optimization is not a technology preference — it is a measurement requirement.
