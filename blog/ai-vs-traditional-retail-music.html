AI vs. Traditional In-Store Music: What's Actually Changed

Daniel Fox · March 2026 · 7 min read

AI-powered retail music optimization is the use of artificial intelligence to generate, deploy, and measure in-store music based on customer behavioral data rather than curated playlists. It represents a structural shift from the catalog-based model that has dominated commercial background music since Muzak's founding in 1934. The difference is not incremental. The underlying architecture—how the music is created, how it is targeted, how it is measured, and who owns it—changes at every level. This article compares the two approaches across five dimensions that matter to retail operators.

Catalog-Based vs. Generative: Where the Music Comes From

Traditional providers—Mood Media, SiriusXM for Business, Soundtrack Your Brand, Rockbot—operate on a catalog model. They license existing recordings from labels and publishers, organize them into playlists or channels, and distribute them to commercial locations. The music was composed and produced for consumer listening (radio, streaming, personal enjoyment). It is repurposed for retail use, not designed for it.

The catalog model introduces three constraints. First, the music was optimized for a listener's emotional experience, not for a retailer's behavioral objectives. A track that sounds great on headphones may do nothing measurable in a store. Second, the catalog is finite. Even a library of two million tracks is a fixed set, and playlists built from it will rotate and repeat. Customers who visit frequently—your most valuable segment—notice repetition. Employees hear it daily and it degrades their environment. Third, every track carries licensing obligations (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, direct label deals), which adds cost and legal complexity.

Generative systems like Entuned produce original music from scratch using AI. There is no catalog. Every composition is new, non-repeating, and built to specification. The music is not repurposed consumer content—it is engineered for the retail environment it will play in. There are no licensing fees because there is nothing to license. The retailer's deployment is the music's only purpose.

One-Size-Fits-All vs. Psychographic Targeting

Traditional services target by genre, era, or mood. A retailer selects "Upbeat Pop" or "Chill Acoustic" or "90s Hits" and the platform delivers a playlist matching that label. This is demographic segmentation applied to music: the assumption that a store's customer base maps to a genre preference, and that genre preference maps to purchase behavior.

The research does not support this assumption at the resolution retailers need. Areni and Kim's 1993 experiment in wine retail found that classical music increased willingness to pay by 8–12% compared to Top 40—but the mechanism was perceived sophistication and congruence with the product category, not genre preference. The genre was a blunt proxy for a precise psychoacoustic effect. A different genre achieving the same harmonic density, spectral profile, and timbral character would likely produce the same result.

Entuned's approach bypasses genre entirely. The platform takes psychographic data about the target customer—lifestyle values, brand affinities, aesthetic preferences, shopping motivations—and translates that profile into 31 discrete musical parameters. The AI then generates music that embodies those parameters. The output might resemble jazz, or ambient electronic, or indie folk, or nothing categorizable at all. The genre is an emergent property of the specification, not the specification itself.

This distinction matters operationally. A traditional provider can give you "Jazz" or "Not Jazz." Entuned can give you music at 72 BPM with moderate harmonic density, high timbral warmth, low rhythmic regularity, and a spectral profile that signals quality without exclusivity—because that specific combination is what the data says will convert your specific customer profile. That level of precision is not available in a catalog-based system at any price.

Playlist Rotation vs. Continuous Generation

A catalog-based service with a 500-track playlist for your store will repeat its full cycle in roughly 30 hours of playback. At 12 operating hours per day, that is a full repeat every 2.5 days. Employees hear the same tracks dozens of times per month. Employee fatigue from repetitive music is one of the most common complaints in retail operations—and one of the least measured.

A generative system does not repeat. Entuned produces a continuous, non-repeating soundtrack. The musical parameters remain consistent (the behavioral targeting holds steady), but the compositions themselves are always new. This eliminates the repetition problem for employees entirely and ensures that even the most frequent customers never hear the same piece twice.

No Measurement vs. Performance Data

This is the most consequential difference. Traditional music providers do not measure the behavioral impact of their playlists. They cannot tell a retailer whether the music playing at 2 PM on Tuesday contributed to or detracted from sales performance. The music is an input with no measured output. It is an expense line, not an investment with a return.

Entuned's architecture is built around measurement. Because the platform controls every parameter of every track it generates, and because it logs what played where and when, it can correlate musical variables with retail outcomes—dwell time, conversion rate, average basket size, customer satisfaction scores. This creates a closed loop: generate, deploy, measure, optimize. Over time, the system learns which parameter configurations produce the best results for each customer profile and store type.

For retail leaders who need to justify every line item, this is the difference between an unmeasurable overhead cost and an investment with documented ROI. How Do Licensing Economics Compare?

Traditional providers pass through licensing costs—either directly (per-track fees, blanket licenses) or embedded in their subscription pricing. A typical Mood Media or Soundtrack Your Brand subscription runs $15–50 per location per month for the software, plus $30–100+ per month in PRO licensing fees (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC) depending on location size and territory. The total cost of licensed music for a mid-size retailer with 50 locations can reach $50,000–90,000 annually—for music that was never designed to move a single business metric.

Entuned's pricing is $299–599 per location per month, which is higher on a per-location basis. But the comparison is misleading without accounting for what each dollar buys. The traditional subscription buys playlist access. Entuned's subscription buys AI-generated music calibrated to your customer psychology, multi-zone deployment, performance measurement, and continuous optimization. The relevant comparison is not cost-per-location but cost-per-outcome. Entuned's model is built on the premise that the subscription should pay for itself through measurable sales uplift within the first 90 days.

What Should Retailers Evaluate?

The decision between traditional and AI-powered retail music is not primarily a technology decision. It is a measurement decision. If a retailer does not intend to measure the impact of in-store audio on business outcomes, a $30/month playlist service is adequate. The music will fill silence. It will not actively harm the business (assuming it is not egregiously mismatched to the customer base). It will cost very little.

If a retailer does intend to treat audio as a performance variable—to measure its effect, optimize it over time, and hold it to the same ROI standard as visual merchandising or digital marketing—then the catalog model is structurally unable to deliver. You cannot optimize what you cannot parameterize. You cannot measure what you do not control. The shift to generative, data-driven in-store audio is not about having better taste in music. It is about having any data at all.
