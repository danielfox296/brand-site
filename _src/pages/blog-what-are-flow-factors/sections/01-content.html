What Are Musical Flow Factors? The 31-Parameter Framework Behind Entuned

Daniel Fox · March 2026 · 8 min read

Flow factors are 31 discrete musical parameters that Entuned uses to specify, generate, and optimize AI-powered retail music. Each factor describes a measurable property of a musical composition—tempo, harmonic density, spectral brightness, rhythmic regularity, dynamic range, timbral warmth, and 25 others—and each maps to one or more verified behavioral outcomes in retail environments. The flow factor framework is Entuned's proprietary method for translating customer psychology into musical specifications that an AI generation system can act on. It is the bridge between "we know what kind of customer shops here" and "here is the exact music that will convert them."

Why Do Flow Factors Exist?

The problem flow factors solve is the translation gap between behavioral science and music production. Four decades of peer-reviewed research—Milliman (1982), Areni and Kim (1993), Chebat and Michon (2003), North, Hargreaves, and McKendrick (1999), and hundreds of subsequent studies—have established that specific musical properties drive specific retail behaviors. Tempo controls movement speed. Harmonic complexity influences perceived quality. Mode (major vs. minor key) affects emotional valence. Spectral brightness shapes arousal levels.

But this research has remained largely inaccessible to retailers because there was no system for operationalizing it. A retail leader who reads that "tempo affects dwell time" cannot call a music provider and say "give me 68 BPM with moderate harmonic density and low spectral brightness." Traditional providers do not think in those terms. They think in genres, playlists, and moods. The translation from scientific finding to commercial music was performed informally, if at all—a music curator's intuition standing in for a systematic framework.

Flow factors replace intuition with specification. They give the behavioral science a language that a generative AI system can execute against, and they give the generative output a vocabulary that behavioral measurement can evaluate.

Why 31 Factors? Not 5, Not 100?

The number 31 is not arbitrary. It reflects the resolution at which musical parameters have been shown to independently influence human perception and behavior in controlled research settings. Fewer factors would collapse meaningfully distinct variables. More would introduce parameters that are either redundant (statistically correlated with existing factors), unmeasurable in practical retail contexts, or unsupported by behavioral evidence.

Consider the extremes. A five-factor model—tempo, volume, genre, major/minor, fast/slow—is what most background music providers implicitly operate on. It is too coarse to capture the difference between "warm and inviting" and "warm and sophisticated," a distinction that matters enormously for a retailer whose brand positioning depends on precisely that nuance. A 100-factor model would include parameters like "degree of swing quantization in the hi-hat pattern" or "reverb tail density in the 2–4 kHz range"—variables that are technically measurable but have no demonstrated behavioral significance in retail contexts.

The 31-factor framework occupies the space where musicological precision meets behavioral relevance. Every factor in the framework satisfies two criteria: it can be independently specified in AI music generation, and it has at least one documented pathway to a behavioral outcome that matters to retailers.

What Are Examples of Flow Factors?

The factors span six broad categories: temporal, harmonic, spectral, rhythmic, dynamic, and timbral. Below are representative examples from each category, along with their behavioral associations.

Tempo (temporal). The speed of the music in beats per minute. This is the single most studied variable in retail music research. Slow tempos (60–75 BPM) reliably increase dwell time through neural entrainment—the body's internal rhythms synchronize to the external pulse. Fast tempos (110–130 BPM) increase movement speed, which can be desirable in high-throughput environments like quick-service restaurants. Entuned specifies tempo to a target range rather than a fixed value, allowing natural variation within behaviorally effective bounds. For a full analysis of tempo's effects and limitations, see Tempo Controls Your Customers' Bodies.

Harmonic density (harmonic). The number of simultaneous pitch relationships in the music—how "thick" or "thin" the chords are. High harmonic density (complex extended chords, multiple simultaneous voices) is associated with perceived sophistication and quality. Low harmonic density (simple triads, open fifths, sparse voicings) is associated with clarity, approachability, and ease. Areni and Kim's finding on classical music and willingness to pay is substantially mediated by harmonic density: classical music tends to have high harmonic density, and it was the density, not the genre label, that signaled quality.

Spectral brightness (spectral). The distribution of energy across the frequency spectrum, specifically the balance between low/mid frequencies and high frequencies. Bright music (more energy in the 4–12 kHz range) feels energetic, alert, and modern. Warm music (more energy below 2 kHz, less above 6 kHz) feels intimate, comfortable, and premium. This factor shapes the perceived energy level of a space independently of tempo—you can have slow, bright music or fast, warm music, and the behavioral effects differ.

Rhythmic regularity (rhythmic). The degree to which the rhythmic pattern is predictable and metrically consistent. High rhythmic regularity (steady beat, predictable accents, four-on-the-floor patterns) supports a sense of order, confidence, and forward motion. Low rhythmic regularity (syncopation, irregular accents, rubato-like timing) creates a more organic, exploratory, contemplative atmosphere. Retail environments that want to encourage decisive purchasing behavior benefit from higher rhythmic regularity. Those that want to encourage open-ended browsing often benefit from lower.

Dynamic range (dynamic). The difference between the quietest and loudest moments in the music. Wide dynamic range (significant contrast between soft and loud passages) creates drama and emotional movement but can be distracting in environments where the music should remain in the background. Narrow dynamic range (relatively consistent volume throughout) produces an even, predictable sonic environment. Most retail applications favor narrower dynamic range than what is typical in consumer music, but the optimal range varies by context.

Timbral warmth (timbral). The overall tonal character of the instrumental palette—specifically, the presence of harmonic overtones in the low-to-mid frequency range that humans perceive as "warmth." High timbral warmth (acoustic instruments, analog-style synthesis, rich overtone content) is associated with comfort, trust, and approachability. Low timbral warmth (digital-sounding synthesis, sparse overtone content, clinical production) can signal modernity and precision but may feel cold in environments where emotional connection matters.

How Do Flow Factors Map to Behavioral Outcomes?

The mapping is not one-to-one. Individual flow factors do not produce isolated behavioral effects. They interact. Tempo affects dwell time, but the magnitude of that effect is modulated by harmonic density, dynamic range, and timbral character. Spectral brightness affects arousal, but in combination with rhythmic regularity, it can either energize or overwhelm. The 31 factors form a parameter space, and it is the configuration of the full space—not any single factor—that produces the behavioral outcome.

This is precisely why a framework is necessary and why genre-based selection fails. Genre is a crude compression of the 31-dimensional space into a single categorical label. "Jazz" implies a particular region of the parameter space (moderate-to-low rhythmic regularity, high harmonic density, moderate timbral warmth), but the variation within jazz is enormous. A Miles Davis ballad and a Buddy Rich big band number occupy completely different behavioral positions. Genre tells you nothing actionable.

Entuned's psychographic-to-musicological translation engine operates in the full parameter space. The engine takes a customer psychographic profile and outputs a target configuration across all 31 factors. The AI generation system then produces music that realizes that configuration. The measurement system evaluates whether the behavioral outcomes match the prediction. When they do not, the engine adjusts. This is AI-powered retail music optimization in its most literal sense: a closed loop between musical parameters and business outcomes.

Why Does This Framework Matter for Retail?

The flow factor framework matters because it makes retail audio auditable, optimizable, and accountable. Without parameterization, music is a black box: something goes in (playlist), something comes out (ambiance), and nobody can explain the relationship between the two. With parameterization, every musical decision has a rationale, every deployment has measurable characteristics, and every outcome can be traced back to a specific configuration of inputs.

For retail leaders, this means audio can finally be managed the same way visual merchandising, lighting, and layout are managed: with data, with testing, with continuous improvement, and with accountability to business outcomes. The 31 flow factors are the unit of analysis that makes this possible. They are what allow a retail operator to say not "our music is good" but "our music is specified to produce these outcomes, and here is the data showing whether it did."

For CFOs and financial leaders, the framework transforms audio from an unaccountable expense line into a measurable investment. When the musical parameters are specified and the outcomes are tracked, the question shifts from "should we pay for music?" to "which parameter configuration produces the highest return?"
